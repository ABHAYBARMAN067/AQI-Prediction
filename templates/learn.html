Understanding Air Quality Index (AQI)

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a valuable tool that helps us understand how clean or polluted the air around us is. It measures the concentration of various harmful pollutants such as particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), carbon monoxide (CO), and ozone (O3). The AQI simplifies this data into a scale that informs us about the potential health risks of the air we breathe daily.

Why is AQI Important?

With growing urbanization and industrialization, air pollution has become a serious concern worldwide. Breathing polluted air can lead to severe health problems, damage the environment, and lower the quality of life.

How Does Air Pollution Affect Our Health?

When AQI is high, it means the air contains elevated levels of pollutants that can deeply penetrate our lungs and bloodstream, causing:

  • Respiratory Issues: Worsening asthma, bronchitis, and other lung diseases.
  • Cardiovascular Effects: Increased risks of heart attacks, strokes, and inflammation.
  • Brain Health: Long-term exposure can impact cognitive function and increase neurological disorder risks.
  • Vulnerable Groups: Children, elderly, pregnant women, and those with existing health conditions are more at risk.

What Can We Do?

Staying updated with daily AQI reports helps us take precautions, like limiting outdoor activities during high pollution days, using air purifiers indoors, and supporting clean energy initiatives.

Governments and environmental organizations also use AQI data to enforce policies to reduce emissions and protect public health.
